What age should be given money?

There isn’t one single “right” age, because access to money works best when it grows with responsibility. 

Most experts agree it should be gradual, not all at once. Here’s a clear, age-based way to think about it:

Ages 5–7: Introduction to Money

Type of access: very limited

· Small amounts of cash or coins

· Learning what money is and that it’s finite

· Simple choices: spend now or save for later

Goal: understand money represents value

Ages 8–11: Guided Practice

Type of access: limited, supervised

· Regular allowance or small earned money

· Simple saving goals

· Adult oversight

Goal: learn basic spending and saving

Ages 12–14: Shared Control

Type of access: partial independence

· Prepaid debit card or youth bank account

· Managing small budgets (clothes, outings)

· Making mistakes with guidance

Goal: practice budgeting and consequences

Ages 15–17: Increasing Independence

Type of access: mostly independent

· Bank account with debit card

· Earnings from part-time work

· Paying for some personal expenses

· Learning about taxes, interest, and fees

Goal: prepare for real-world money management.

 

 

Full Legal Control

Ages 18+

Type of access: independent

· Full control over bank accounts

· Credit cards and loans (with caution)

· Rent, bills, and long-term financial decisions

Goal: manage money independently and responsibly

Key Principle (More Important Than Age)

The “right” age depends more on:

· Maturity and self-control

· Understanding consequences

· Guidance and education
